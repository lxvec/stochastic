User: im trying to understand girsanovs theorem but first i need to understand what it means to have two brownian motions under different measures

Assistant: The key point is that “Brownian motion” is not just a property of a function t ↦ W_t(ω). It is a property of the law of that process under a particular probability measure.

So when people say “Brownian motion under P” versus “Brownian motion under Q,” they mean:
- same filtered space (Ω,F,(F_t)),
- maybe even the same sample paths,
- but a different probability measure, so the random process has a different distributional behavior.

1. What does it mean for a process to be Brownian under a measure?
A process W=(W_t)_{t≥0} is a Brownian motion under P if, under P,
- W_0=0 a.s.,
- paths are continuous,
- increments are independent,
- W_t-W_s ~ N(0,t-s) for t>s.

All of that is measure-dependent.

So the sentence “W is a Brownian motion under P” means those properties hold when probabilities are computed with P.

If you switch to another measure Q, the same process W_t may no longer have mean-zero Gaussian increments, so it may stop being Brownian.

2. Same process, different measure
A very common situation is:
- under P, W_t is Brownian;
- under Q, the same process W_t has a drift.

For example, suppose under Q,
W_t = W̃_t + μ t,
where W̃ is Brownian under Q.

Then:
- under P: W is Brownian;
- under Q: W is not Brownian, because W_t-W_s ~ N(μ(t-s), t-s), so the increments are no longer centered;
- but under Q, the shifted process W̃_t := W_t - μ t is Brownian.

This is exactly the kind of thing Girsanov formalizes.

3. Why this is not contradictory
It can feel strange because the sample path t ↦ W_t(ω) is the same function regardless of whether you say “under P” or “under Q.”

What changes is how likely different paths are.

Think of Ω as a set of continuous paths. Then:
- P assigns one weighting to these paths,
- Q assigns another weighting.

Under one weighting, the coordinate process may look like standard Brownian motion.
Under another weighting, the same coordinate process may look like Brownian motion plus drift.

So the path itself does not change. The law changes.

4. Canonical picture
A very clean way to think about it is on path space:
- Ω = C([0,∞),R),
- X_t(ω)=ω(t) is the coordinate process.

Then:
- under Wiener measure P, X is standard Brownian motion;
- under another measure Q, the same coordinate process X might be Brownian with drift.

So “Brownian motion under different measures” often means:
- the same coordinate process,
- but different laws.

5. What Girsanov says in this language
Start with W Brownian under P.

Define a new measure Q by a density process of the form
(dQ/dP)|_{F_t} = exp(∫_0^t θ_s dW_s - (1/2)∫_0^t θ_s^2 ds),
assuming conditions like Novikov so this is valid.

Then Girsanov says that under Q,
W̃_t := W_t - ∫_0^t θ_s ds
is a Brownian motion.

So:
- under P, W is Brownian;
- under Q, W typically is not Brownian anymore;
- under Q, the drift-corrected process W̃ is Brownian.

That is the main meaning of “two Brownian motions under different measures.”

6. A simple constant-drift example
Take θ_s ≡ μ constant. Then
W̃_t = W_t - μ t.

Interpretation:
- under P, W_t is standard Brownian;
- define Q with the exponential martingale density;
- under Q, W̃_t is standard Brownian;
- equivalently, under Q, W_t = W̃_t + μ t, so W has drift μ.

So the measure change “moves drift” between the process and the probability measure.

7. The most important conceptual sentence
A process is not “Brownian motion” absolutely. It is Brownian motion relative to a measure.

That is why it makes sense to say:
- W is Brownian under P,
- W̃ is Brownian under Q,

even when W and W̃ live on the same filtered space and are related by
W̃_t = W_t - ∫_0^t θ_s ds.

8. What you should keep in mind before learning Girsanov
Before Girsanov, the right mental model is:
1. Fix a filtered probability space.
2. A measure P tells you how likely events are.
3. “Brownian motion” means a process has the Brownian law under that measure.
4. Changing the measure changes the law.
5. Under the new measure, the old Brownian motion may gain a drift, but a shifted version becomes Brownian again.

Assistant: Yes — and this is exactly where the notation can become misleading.

The cleanest way to think about it
Start with one process W=(W_t)_{t≥0} on one filtered space (Ω,F,(F_t)).

Assume:
- under P, W is a Brownian motion.

Now change measure from P to Q.

At this point, the sample paths of W have not changed. It is still the same random variable W_t(ω) for each t,ω.

What changes is:
- which paths are considered more likely,
- so the law of W changes.

So under Q, the same process W usually is not Brownian anymore.

Where W^P and W^Q come from
Many books write:
- W^P: a Brownian motion under P,
- W^Q: a Brownian motion under Q.

These are often different names for related processes, not necessarily two unrelated objects.

A very common convention is:
W^P_t := W_t,
W^Q_t := W_t - ∫_0^t θ_s ds.

Then:
- W^P is Brownian under P,
- W^Q is Brownian under Q.

So in that convention, yes, they are different processes as functions of t, because one is the other minus a drift term.

But they are built from the same underlying W.

Anchor in the P-Brownian motion
Let’s do the constant drift case first, since it shows everything transparently.

Start with W Brownian under P.

Define a new measure Q on F_t by
(dQ/dP)|_{F_t} = exp(μ W_t - (1/2) μ^2 t).

This is the exponential tilt.

Now ask:
How does the same W_t look under Q?

Under P,
W_t ~ N(0,t).

Under Q, compute its moment generating function:
E_Q[e^{λ W_t}] = E_P[e^{λ W_t} dQ/dP] = E_P[e^{(λ+μ)W_t - (1/2)μ^2 t}].

Since under P, W_t~N(0,t),
E_P[e^{aW_t}] = e^{(1/2)a^2 t}.

So
E_Q[e^{λ W_t}] = e^{-(1/2)μ^2 t} e^{(1/2)(λ+μ)^2 t} = e^{μ λ t + (1/2) λ^2 t}.

But that is exactly the mgf of N(μ t,t).

So under Q,
W_t ~ N(μ t,t).

That means: under Q, the process W has picked up drift μ.

So the same process that was Brownian under P now looks like
W_t = “Brownian part” + μ t
under Q.

Why subtracting the drift works
Now define
W̃_t := W_t - μ t.

Under Q, since W_t~N(μ t,t), we get
W̃_t ~ N(0,t).

More importantly, for 0≤s<t,
W̃_t-W̃_s = (W_t-W_s)-μ(t-s).

Under Q, the increment W_t-W_s has law N(μ(t-s), t-s), so
W̃_t-W̃_s ~ N(0,t-s).

And Girsanov tells you the increments remain independent under Q, so W̃ is a Brownian motion under Q.

So the slogan is:
Under Q, W = W̃ + μ t,
where W̃ is Brownian under Q.

What is really happening conceptually?
You start with:
- under P, W is standard Brownian.

Then you tilt the measure so that paths with positive trend become more likely.

So under Q, the same coordinate process W now tends to drift upward.

That is why under Q, W is no longer centered.

Subtract off that deterministic drift, and what remains is the centered random fluctuation part again — that is the Q-Brownian motion.

A good notation map
This is often the safest way to read it:
- W: the original process on the space.
- Under P: W is Brownian.
- Under Q: W is Brownian with drift.
- Define W^Q_t := W_t - ∫_0^t θ_s ds. Then W^Q is Brownian under Q.

Some authors call the original one W^P, so then W^Q_t = W^P_t - ∫_0^t θ_s ds.

So:
- W^P and W^Q are usually different processes,
- but they are directly related,
- and both live on the same space.

One-sentence summary
You should think:
Start with one Brownian motion W under P. After changing to Q, the same W typically gains a drift. Subtract that drift, and the resulting process is the Brownian motion under Q.

Assistant: Yes. Here is the cleanest numerical example.

1. Start under P
Let W=(W_t)_{t≥0} be a standard Brownian motion under P.

Pick a constant drift μ = 2.

So under P,
- W_1 ~ N(0,1),
- W_2-W_1 ~ N(0,1),
- hence W_2 ~ N(0,2).

2. Define the new measure Q
We define Q by
(dQ/dP)|_{F_t} = Z_t := exp(μ W_t - (1/2) μ^2 t).

With μ=2, this is
Z_t = exp(2W_t - 2t).

At time t=1,
Z_1 = exp(2W_1 - 2).

At time t=2,
Z_2 = exp(2W_2 - 4).

This means: paths with large positive W_t get weighted more heavily under Q, and paths with negative W_t get weighted less.

3. What does the same W look like under Q?
Under Q, the same process W is no longer standard Brownian. It now has drift 2.

So under Q,
- W_1 ~ N(2,1),
- W_2-W_1 ~ N(2,1),
- W_t-W_s ~ N(2(t-s), t-s).

So the same process that was centered under P is shifted upward under Q.

4. A very concrete numeric path
Suppose one realized path has
W_0=0, W_1=1.7, W_2=3.9.

This is the same path whether you talk about P or Q. Only its probability changes.

Its likelihood weight at time 2 is
Z_2 = exp(2·3.9 - 4) = exp(3.8) ≈ 44.7.

So under Q, this upward-trending path is much more likely than under P.

Take another path with endpoint W_2 = -1. Then
Z_2 = exp(2·(-1)-4)=e^{-6}≈0.0025.

So under Q, downward paths get heavily downweighted.

That is the intuition: Q favors paths that look like they have positive drift.

5. Why subtracting drift gives Brownian under Q
Define
W̃_t := W_t - 2t.

For the sample path above,
- W̃_1 = 1.7 - 2 = -0.3,
- W̃_2 = 3.9 - 4 = -0.1.

Now under Q,
- W_1~N(2,1), so W̃_1=W_1-2 ~ N(0,1),
- W_2-W_1~N(2,1), so W̃_2-W̃_1=(W_2-W_1)-2 ~ N(0,1).

So after subtracting 2t, the increments become centered again.

That is exactly why W̃_t = W_t - 2t is Brownian under Q.

6. Even simpler: one-time random variable version
Before the process version, it helps to see the one-variable analogue.

Let X~N(0,1) under P.

Define dQ/dP = exp(μ X - (1/2)μ^2).

Then under Q,
X~N(μ,1).

So the exponential tilt changes a centered Gaussian into a shifted Gaussian.

Brownian Girsanov is exactly this, but applied consistently across all times t.

7. Is the exponential needed?
For the standard theorem: yes, essentially.

Why? Because you are changing the measure by a Radon–Nikodym derivative, so you need a nonnegative random variable Z_t with expectation 1. The exponential gives exactly the right likelihood ratio to shift a Gaussian mean.

For a single Gaussian X~N(0,1), the density ratio between N(μ,1) and N(0,1) is
exp(-(x-μ)^2/2) / exp(-x^2/2) = exp(μ x - (1/2)μ^2).

So the exponential is not arbitrary. It is the exact density ratio that shifts the mean.

For Brownian motion, the analogous likelihood ratio over time is
exp(∫_0^t θ_s dW_s - (1/2)∫_0^t θ_s^2 ds).

That is the process version of Gaussian mean-shifting.

So:
- not “exponential because magic,”
- but “exponential because Gaussian likelihood ratios are exponential.”

8. What to remember
Anchor everything in this picture:
- Under P, W is standard Brownian.
- Define Q by exponential tilting.
- Under Q, the same W has drift.
- Subtract the drift: W_t^Q := W_t - μ t, and W^Q is Brownian under Q.

So in the constant case,
W_t = W_t^Q + μ t under Q.

The clean version

- W is a martingale,
- E_P[W_t | F_s] = W_s.

- the probability measure,
- therefore the conditional expectations,
- therefore the martingale property.

A process can be a martingale under one measure and fail to be one under another.
