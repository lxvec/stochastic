Cox–Ingersoll–Ross Short-Rate Model

Distribution of rT — empirical vs. theoretical (non-central χ²)

The SDE drt = κ(θ − rt)dt + σ√rt dWt admits a strong solution by the Yamada–Watanabe theorem, which extends the classical Lipschitz theory to certain Hölder-continuous diffusions.

  • Drift b(r) = κ(θ − r) is globally Lipschitz: |b(r) − b(r')| = κ|r − r'|.
  • Diffusion σ(r) = σ√r is not Lipschitz at r = 0, but satisfies a Hölder-½ condition: |σ√r − σ√r'| ≤ σ√|r − r'|.
  • Yamada–Watanabe guarantees pathwise uniqueness when the diffusion coefficient satisfies |σ(r) − σ(r')| ≤ h(|r − r'|) with ∫₀¹ h(u)⁻² du = ∞. For h(u) = σ√u: ∫₀¹ (σ²u)⁻¹ du = ∞. ✓
  • Pathwise uniqueness + weak existence (from martingale problem) ⟹ strong solution.

Strong means rt is a measurable functional of the driving Brownian motion W — the randomness comes entirely from W, not from any additional independent source.

We require κ > 0, θ > 0, σ > 0, and r0 > 0. The key additional constraint is the Feller condition:

κ — Mean-Reversion Speed
The drift κ(θ − rt) is a restoring force. Half-life of deviations from θ is ln(2)/κ years. Large κ → fast pull-back; small κ → persistent excursions.
θ — Long-Run Mean
The equilibrium short rate. As t → ∞, E[rt] → θ and the stationary distribution is Gamma(2κθ/σ², 2κ/σ²) with mean θ.
σ — Volatility (vol-of-rate)
Instantaneous variance = σ²rtdt. The square-root scaling means absolute volatility shrinks toward zero as rt → 0 — a key empirical feature.
Feller Condition: 2κθ ≥ σ²
This ensures the boundary r = 0 is inaccessible — rt > 0 almost surely. Equivalently, the degrees-of-freedom parameter d = 4κθ/σ² ≥ 2. When violated (d < 2), zero is accessible but reflecting. Drag σ upward with the slider and watch the Feller indicator turn red — paths will start grazing 0.

Conditional on r0, the rescaled process follows a non-central chi-squared distribution:

c · rt ~ χ²(d, λ(t))

c = 4κ / [σ²(1 − e−κt)]  (scaling constant)
d = 4κθ / σ²      (degrees of freedom; = 2 × Feller ratio)
λ(t) = c · r₀ · e−κt     (non-centrality; → 0 as t → ∞)
Conditional moments:
  • E[rt] = r₀e−κt + θ(1 − e−κt)
  • Var[rt] = r₀(σ²/κ)(e−κt − e−2κt)
               + θ(σ²/2κ)(1 − e−κt)²
Stationary distribution (t → ∞):
  • r∞ ~ Gamma(α = 2κθ/σ², β = 2κ/σ²)
  • Mean: θ    Variance: θσ²/(2κ)
  • λ(t) → 0, so non-central → central χ²(d) → Gamma

Hit Run on the distribution chart above to compare the empirical histogram of rT (across independent simulated paths) against the exact theoretical non-central χ² density — they should align closely for large sample counts.

  • Mean-reversion: The drift κ(θ − rt) provides a restoring force. Rates are pulled toward the long-run level θ, consistent with economic equilibrium and the empirical observation that rates do not drift without bound.
  • Non-negativity: Under the Feller condition 2κθ ≥ σ², rt > 0 a.s., ruling out negative nominal rates in the model.
  • Level-dependent volatility: Instantaneous variance σ²rtdt implies that when rates are low, absolute fluctuations are small — more realistic than the Vasicek model's constant vol, which can generate negative rates.
  • Affine structure: Zero-coupon bond prices have closed form P(t,T) = A(t,T)·exp(−B(t,T)·rt), where A and B satisfy Riccati ODEs. This makes the model analytically tractable for derivatives pricing.
  • Ergodicity: The process has a unique invariant (stationary) distribution Gamma(2κθ/σ², 2κ/σ²) toward which the marginals converge. Useful for long-horizon calibration.
  • Distributional tractability: The non-central χ² distribution has known moments and CDF (via regularised incomplete gamma), enabling likelihood-based estimation.

Bottom line: CIR is the canonical square-root diffusion for interest rates. It passes the three minimal tests for a short-rate model: (1) rates mean-revert, (2) rates stay non-negative, and (3) volatility scales with the level. Its affine structure keeps calibration and pricing tractable — the benchmark model before multi-factor extensions (A₁(3), etc.).
